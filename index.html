Our stance

We are not anti-school. We are pro-accountability.

Slough has hard-working teachers doing an extraordinary job in difficult conditions. Nothing on this site is aimed at them. It is aimed at the people who make decisions about our children — and who are already bound, by law, by regulation and by their own codes of conduct, to explain themselves.

Praise for a good school is as welcome here as criticism of a bad one, and we publish both.

The people who run them — headteachers, governors, academy trustees — hold public office. They are required to be open, accountable and honest.

The Seven Principles of Public Life

The Nolan Principles

Everyone who holds public office in England — including the people who run our schools — is bound by these seven standards. Knowing them is the first step to holding anyone to them.

I

Selflessness

Holders of public office should act solely in terms of the public interest.

II

Integrity

Holders of public office must avoid placing themselves under any obligation to people or organisations that might try inappropriately to influence them in their work. They should not act or take decisions in order to gain financial or other material benefits for themselves, their family, or their friends. They must declare and resolve any interests and relationships.

III

Objectivity

Holders of public office must act and take decisions impartially, fairly and on merit, using the best evidence and without discrimination or bias.

IV

Accountability

Holders of public office are accountable to the public for their decisions and actions and must submit themselves to the scrutiny necessary to ensure this.

V

Openness

Holders of public office should act and take decisions in an open and transparent manner. Information should not be withheld from the public unless there are clear and lawful reasons for so doing.

VI

Honesty

Holders of public office should be truthful.

VII

Leadership

Holders of public office should exhibit these principles in their own behaviour. They should actively promote and robustly support the principles and be willing to challenge poor behaviour wherever it occurs.

How to complain — step by step

The complaints ladder

The full escalation route for a Slough school. Each rung has to be climbed in order — skip one and you hand them a reason to send you back to the bottom.

Start here
1

Informal — the school

Raise it with the class teacher or head of year. Keep it in writing so there's a record.

2

Formal complaint — the school

Follow the school's published complaints policy exactly. Every school in England must have one and must publish it.

3

Governors' panel

If you're not satisfied, escalate to a panel of governors, who consider the complaint independently of the head.

4

Ofsted

Ofsted can consider complaints that point to wider or safeguarding failures across a school.

Top rung
5

Department for Education

Academies and trusts answer to the DfE / ESFA; maintained schools may go to the Local Government Ombudsman.

Put it in writing

A phone call won't prove what was said; an email will. Schools often ring rather than write — always ask for the details to be confirmed in writing.

Follow the procedure

Every route above the school will ask you to show you completed the school's own process first. Get the complaints policy and follow it to the letter.

Your legal rights

Your family life has legal protection that no school policy can simply override. Article 8 of the Human Rights Act protects your private and family life, and where a school or council interferes with it, they must be able to show it was lawful, necessary and proportionate. This guide also covers school records — what's held about your child, and what you're entitled to see.
